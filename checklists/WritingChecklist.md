# Writing checklist

## Brief and audience

- [ ] The approved brief identifies one primary reader, intent, outcome, query, and call to action.
- [ ] The article delivers the promise made by its title and introduction.
- [ ] Scope is narrow enough to finish without unrelated sections.

## Structure and clarity

- [ ] One descriptive H1 is followed by logical H2 and H3 sections.
- [ ] The introduction states what the reader will learn, build, or decide.
- [ ] Headings are specific and useful when scanned alone.
- [ ] Steps are chronological; prerequisites appear before dependent actions.
- [ ] Paragraphs are concise, lists are parallel, and unnecessary jargon is removed.
- [ ] Every technical term is defined before it is relied upon.

## Technical quality

- [ ] Pin numbers, voltages, component values, units, filenames, and code match throughout.
- [ ] Instructions distinguish power-off wiring from powered testing.
- [ ] Expected results are observable or measurable.
- [ ] Troubleshooting begins with safe, likely checks.
- [ ] Claims are supported by a bench test, datasheet, or authoritative reference.
- [ ] Limitations and uncertainty are stated plainly.

## Voice and trust

- [ ] Tone is practical, encouraging, precise, and appropriate for beginners.
- [ ] The article avoids hype, manufactured urgency, and unsupported superlatives.
- [ ] Product mentions explain learner fit rather than commission opportunity.
- [ ] Safety warnings are actionable and proportionate.
- [ ] Spelling, grammar, capitalization, terminology, and units are consistent.

## Handoff

- [ ] Template prompts, editorial notes, and unresolved markers are removed.
- [ ] Links, media callouts, metadata, and reviewer questions are clearly marked for production.
- [ ] Technical and editorial reviewers have signed off.
